1. KOALA
========

Hazard shown before choices:
  A frightened, injured or cornered koala can scratch with powerful claws.
  Keep people and dogs well back. Do not stand between it and a tree.
  [WRM VERIFY preferred local wording]

What is the koala doing?
  |
  +-- On the ground
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Bring dogs and people inside or well away.
  |     -> Keep the exact tree or spot in view.
  |     -> Do not pick it up, crowd it or block its escape.
  |
  +-- Hit or visibly injured
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Stay out of traffic; use hazard lights only from a safe position.
  |     -> Note where it is and which way it moves.
  |     -> Do not chase or handle it.
  |
  +-- In a yard with dogs
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Bring every dog inside.
  |     -> Watch from inside if possible.
  |     -> Leave the koala a clear route to a tree.
  |
  +-- In a tree and looks well
        -> LOG / WATCH
        -> Photograph only from the existing safe distance.
        -> Note the tree, time and behaviour.

2. KANGAROO
============

Hazard shown before choices:
  Kangaroos are large, powerful wild animals. An injured or cornered kangaroo can kick,
  scratch and cause severe or fatal injury. Do not approach, chase or corner it.

Which best describes the kangaroo?
  |
  +-- Alive and cannot get up
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Keep people and dogs well away.
  |     -> Keep its location in view from safety.
  |     -> Do not check the pouch or attempt to move it.
  |
  +-- Moving but hurt
  |     -> CALL NOW - MOBILE CASE
  |     -> Note direction, speed, visible injuries and each changed location.
  |     -> Do not follow closely or chase it.
  |
  +-- Dead or not moving
  |     -> CALL NOW - POSSIBLE POUCH YOUNG
  |     -> Mark the exact location and stay safely clear of traffic.
  |     -> Do not approach to confirm death, check the pouch or move the animal.
  |
  +-- Acting aggressively or blocking a safe exit
  |     -> HUMAN SAFETY FIRST
  |     -> Increase distance slowly and put a solid barrier between people and the animal.
  |     -> Do not run at it, turn the encounter into a rescue, or strike it.
  |     -> Call 000 if a person is in immediate danger.
  |     -> [WRM VERIFY local dangerous-animal routing]
  |
  +-- Looks healthy
        -> LOG / WATCH
        -> Give it space. Do not approach or feed it.

6. SNAKE
========

FIRST SAFETY GATE
  |
  +-- Could anyone have been bitten?
        -> Call 000 immediately.
        -> Keep the person still.
        -> Do not wash the bite or try to catch or kill the snake.
        -> Follow the 000 call-taker's first-aid directions.

Possible identification - only from where the reporter already stands:
  |
  +-- Not sure / cannot see safely
  |     -> Record POSSIBLE ID: UNKNOWN
  |     -> Treat as dangerous.
  |
  +-- Sea snake
  |     Clues: in sea, surf or washed ashore; often a flattened paddle-like tail.
  |     -> Treat as highly venomous.
  |     -> Never touch it, return it to water or assume it is dead.
  |
  +-- Eastern brown or brown-looking snake
  |     Clues: often slender; colour may be brown, orange, grey or almost black.
  |     -> Treat as highly venomous.
  |     -> Colour cannot confirm identification.
  |
  +-- Red-bellied black or similar black snake
  |     Clues: glossy black; red or pink may show on lower sides; often near water.
  |     -> Treat as highly venomous.
  |     -> Other dangerous snakes can look similar.
  |
  +-- Carpet python
  |     Clues: usually heavy-bodied with repeated cream and dark blotches.
  |     -> Possible non-venomous match, never a safety clearance.
  |     -> Keep back and use a trained catcher.
  |
  +-- Common or green tree snake
  |     Clues: very slender and agile, long whip-like tail; colour varies widely.
  |     -> Possible non-venomous match, never a safety clearance.
  |     -> Keep back and use a trained catcher.
  |
  +-- Other / does not match
        -> Treat as dangerous.

Where is the snake?
  |
  +-- Inside a building
  |     -> DANGER / CALL NOW
  |     -> Leave the room if safe, close the internal door, move people and pets away.
  |     -> Watch likely exits from a secure distance.
  |     -> Do not trap, follow, touch or photograph it up close.
  |
  +-- Yard, campsite or public space
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Bring children and pets inside and warn others from a distance.
  |     -> Do not catch, move or block it.
  |
  +-- Road or bush
  |     -> GIVE IT A WIDE PATH
  |     -> Let it move away. Call if injured or unable to leave.
  |
  +-- Beach, rocks or water
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Keep people, children and dogs away from the waterline.
  |     -> Note water / surf / high-tide-line position.
  |     -> Do not touch it, return it to water or stand between it and open water.
  |
  +-- Injured or trapped
        -> CALL NOW
        -> Keep everyone back regardless of the possible species.
        -> Do not attempt a rescue.

12. PELICAN OR LARGE WATERBIRD
==============================

Classification:
  A pelican is a waterbird. It is not a bird of prey.
  It gets its own app tile because risk and rescue questions matter more than taxonomy.

Hazard shown before choices:
  A distressed pelican's long beak and powerful wings can injure a person's face or eyes.

What is wrong?
  |
  +-- Hooked or tangled
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Keep people and dogs away. Note trailing line and where it moves.
  |     -> Do not grab its beak, wings or neck.
  |     -> Do not cut trailing line or pull a hook.
  |
  +-- Cannot fly or visibly hurt
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Keep a clear circle around it. Note whether a wing is drooping.
  |     -> Do not chase, cover or throw it into the air.
  |
  +-- Oiled or looks sick
  |     -> CALL NOW
  |     -> Keep the area quiet.
  |     -> Do not wash, feed or handle it.
  |
  +-- Healthy sighting
        -> LOG / WATCH
        -> Keep dogs away and let it move freely.

DETAILS TO COLLECT AFTER THE SAFETY ACTION
==========================================

Only collect these when the reporter can do so without moving closer or delaying a call:

  Exact current location
    - GPS from the reporter's phone
    - map point
    - beach access number, road, house, campsite or landmark
    - whether the animal has moved since first seen

  Time
    - time first seen
    - time of latest confirmed sighting

  Animal
    - selected group
    - possible species only if safely visible
    - number of animals
    - approximate size

  Situation
    - alive / dead / not sure
    - stationary / mobile and direction
    - visible injury or unusual behaviour
    - trapped, hooked, tangled or contaminated
    - nearby road, water, tide, powerline, fire, dogs, cats, people or boats

  Evidence
    - photo or short video only from the existing safe position
    - never require a photo
    - preserve the original file, time and GPS if available

  Contact
    - reporter name and callback number, optional
    - whether the reporter can safely remain nearby
