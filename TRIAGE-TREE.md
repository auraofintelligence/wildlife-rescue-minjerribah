# The triage tree

**DRAFT for Wildlife Rescue Minjerribah to mark up.** Nothing here is final until the team's
trained, licensed people have signed it off. The app shows this content to the public, so every
word below should be treated the way a 000 script is treated: reviewed, agreed, owned by the
people with the training.

## The rules the tree obeys

1. **One question per screen.** Big buttons, icon plus a few words, never a form.
2. **A question exists only if the answer changes who gets called or what the person should do
   in the next sixty seconds.** Everything else is captured silently or asked later.
3. **"Not sure" is always an answer, and it always routes to the safest assumption.** An
   unidentified snake is treated as a king brown. The app never rewards moving closer.
4. **The public is never asked to rescue, handle, contain or touch an animal.** Wildlife rescue
   takes training, licensing and insurance. Public action cards may only use these verbs:
   watch, keep back (people, dogs, cats, drones), call, stay, mark the spot, photograph from a
   distance, turn lights off, keep pets inside. The only hands-on instructions allowed concern
   the person's own safety (hazard lights, washing a wound).
5. **Call is always on screen.** Every screen carries the Wildlife Rescue Minjerribah call
   button, 0448 466 556. The questions serve the call; they never gate it.
6. **The path fills in the report.** By the time an action card is reached, animal, situation,
   danger, urgency and likely cause are already recorded. The person only adds a photo,
   location and their contact if they're willing.

## The animal tiles

Fifteen tiles, one tap each. Kangaroo and wallaby are separate (size changes the danger and the
handling crew). Snakes get their own identification step. Goannas and blue-tongues get their own
tile so they stop being reported as snakes.

## The paths

Format: question → answers → **ACTION CARD** (urgency, do, don't, call). All cards also say:
stay if it's safe, mark the spot, photo from a distance.

### Koala
What's happening? → on the ground / hit or injured / in a yard with dogs / up a tree, looks well
- On the ground, hit, or dogs → **NOW.** A koala on the ground is unwell until proven
  otherwise. Do: keep dogs inside, keep people back, note the exact tree or spot. Don't: pick
  it up, crowd it. Call the team.
- Looks well → **LOG IT.** Healthy sightings build the population map. Photo from a distance.

### Kangaroo
Is it alive? → alive / dead / not sure (not sure = alive)
- Alive → can it get up? → can't get up / moving but hurt
  - Either → **NOW.** Do: keep well back (a cornered roo kicks), keep dogs away, hazard lights
    if on a road, note which way it moves if it leaves. Don't: corner or chase it. Call.
    "Moving but hurt" marks the case as mobile: the map shows keep-an-eye-out.
- Dead → on a road? → yes / no
  - **NOW, even though it has died.** A joey may be alive in the pouch, and only a trained
    carer may check. Do: hazard lights, stay if safe, mark the spot exactly. Don't: reach into
    the pouch, move the animal. Call. (Road answer records vehicle strike as the cause.)

### Wallaby
Same path as kangaroo, gentler warning ("keep back, let it settle"), same joey rule.

### Echidna
What's happening? → hurt or on a road / dug itself in / wandering, looks fine
- Hurt or road → **NOW.** Do: mark the exact spot (carers must know where it was found), keep
  dogs away. Don't: dig it out, move it, put anything over it. Call.
- Dug in → **SOON.** It's frightened, not stuck. Keep everyone back and call; the team decides.
- Fine → **LOG IT.**

### Possum or glider
What's happening? → out in daylight or on the ground / caught by a cat or dog / baby alone /
healthy at night
- Daylight, ground, cat or dog → **NOW.** A cat-caught possum needs care even if it looks
  fine. Do: keep the pet inside, watch from a distance. Don't: touch or box it. Call.
- Baby alone → **NOW.** Do: keep watch from inside, keep pets in. Don't: touch it or move it;
  mum may be nearby. Call.
- Healthy → **LOG IT.**

### Snake
Which is the closest match? (judged only from where the person already stands; never move
closer) → not sure / sea snake / eastern brown or brown-looking snake / red-bellied black
or similar black snake / carpet python / common or green tree snake / other

- **Not sure / cannot see safely** is the first and most prominent answer. Treat as dangerous.
- **Sea snake:** in the sea, surf or washed ashore; often a flattened, paddle-shaped tail.
  Treat as highly venomous. Never touch it, push it into the water or assume it is dead.
- **Eastern brown or brown-looking:** often slender and fast-moving, but colour may be brown,
  orange, grey or almost black. Colour cannot identify it. Treat as highly venomous.
- **Red-bellied black or similar black snake:** glossy black, sometimes red or pink visible
  along the lower sides; often near water. Other dangerous snakes look similar. Treat as
  highly venomous.
- **Carpet python:** usually heavy-bodied, olive to brown, with cream and dark blotches in a
  repeated pattern. Non-venomous, but still keep back and use a trained catcher.
- **Common or green tree snake:** very slender and agile with a whip-like tail; colour varies
  from green and olive to brown, black or blue. Non-venomous, but identification remains
  uncertain and the person must still keep back.
- **Other / does not match:** treat as dangerous. South-east Queensland has many additional
  species, including highly venomous snakes.

The identification is recorded as a **possible match**, never a confirmed species. The action
does not become less cautious after a non-venomous match.

Where is it? → inside a building / yard or campsite / road or bush / beach, rocks or water /
it's injured
- Inside → **NOW, DANGER.** Do: everyone and pets out of the room, shut the internal door,
  watch the exits from a distance. Don't: try to trap, follow or photograph it up close. Call
  the team; they bring in the licensed catcher. If anyone has been bitten: 000 immediately.
- Yard or campsite → **NOW.** Keep pets and children inside, watch from a window. Call.
- Road or bush → **SOON.** Give it space; it's passing through. Call if it's injured or won't
  move on. Every venomous sighting is still worth logging.
- Beach, rocks or water → **NOW.** Keep people, children and dogs away from the waterline.
  Note whether it is in the water, surf or above the high-tide line. Don't touch it, return it
  to the water or stand between it and open water. Call.
- Injured → **NOW.** Keep right back regardless of species. Call.

Every snake action card begins: **Possible bite? Call 000 immediately. Keep the person still.
Do not wash the bite or try to catch the snake.**

### Goanna or blue-tongue
What's happening? → hurt / stuck or trapped / just visiting
- Hurt or trapped → **NOW.** Keep dogs in, keep back, call.
- Visiting → **LOG IT.** They're locals. Keep dogs inside until it moves on.

### Sea turtle
What's happening? → washed up or stranded / tangled in line or net / hatchlings out /
swimming oddly
- Stranded or tangled → **NOW.** Do: keep crowds, dogs and drones back, note the exact beach
  spot. Don't: drag it to the water, pull hooks or line, pour anything on it. Call the team;
  the stranding hotline 1300 130 372 is shown alongside.
- Hatchlings → **NOW.** Do: turn off nearby lights, keep the path to the water clear of people
  and dogs. Don't: pick them up or shepherd them. Call.
- Swimming oddly → **KEEP WATCH.** Photo, direction it's heading, call.

### Dolphin, whale or dugong
Any answer (stranded / tangled / distressed in shallows / dead) → **NOW.** Do: keep well back,
they're powerful even beached; keep dogs and drones away; note landmarks. Don't: push it back
out, hold it upright. Call the team plus the stranding hotline.

### Seabird or shorebird
What's happening? → hooked or tangled in fishing line / can't fly / oiled or sick / chick alone

Most bird calls on the island are fishing gear, so it leads.
- Hooked or tangled → is it stuck where it is? → stuck / loose and trailing line
  - Stuck (tethered to jetty, snag or rocks) → **NOW.** Do: keep people back so it doesn't
    thrash its wing worse, keep the line slack if that means simply not touching it. Don't:
    cut it free (a bird that flies off trailing line dies slowly out of sight), don't pull the
    hook. Call.
  - Loose → **KEEP WATCH.** Marked mobile. Do: watch which way it goes, photo. Call.
- Can't fly, oiled, sick → **NOW.** Keep back, keep dogs leashed, call.
- Chick alone → **KEEP WATCH.** Parents are usually nearby. Watch from a distance, keep dogs
  leashed, call if it's still alone or clearly hurt.

### Land bird
What's happening? → hit a window or car / caught by a cat or dog / baby bird on the ground /
tangled or hooked
- Window or car → **SOON.** Stunned birds often recover. Watch from inside, keep pets in, call
  if it hasn't moved on within the hour or looks hurt.
- Cat or dog → **NOW.** Always urgent even if it looks fine. Keep the pet inside, call.
- Baby bird → feathered and hopping / bald or eyes closed
  - Feathered → **KEEP WATCH.** Probably a fledgling learning to fly with parents close.
    Keep pets in, watch from a distance, call if it's hurt or alone at dusk.
  - Bald → **NOW.** Call.
- Tangled or hooked → **NOW.** Same rules as seabirds: never cut it free. Call.

### Bird of prey
What's happening? → hurt or grounded / tangled or caught / healthy sighting
- Hurt or tangled → **NOW.** Do: stay well back (talons), keep spectators and dogs off, exact
  landmark. Don't: approach or cover it. Call.
- Healthy → **LOG IT.**

### Flying-fox or bat
What's happening? → on the ground or hurt / caught on a fence or netting / someone was
scratched or bitten / several look sick or dead
- Any animal answer → **NOW, DANGER.** Do: keep everyone and every pet right away, watch from
  a distance. Don't: touch it, even to help, even if it looks dead. Only vaccinated rescuers
  may handle bats. Call.
- Someone scratched or bitten → **EMERGENCY.** Wash the wound thoroughly with soap and running
  water now, then seek urgent medical care (000 or a doctor immediately) and tell the team.
- Several sick or dead → **NOW.** Keep clear, call; this can matter for animal and human
  health monitoring.

### Other / Not sure
**NOW if it looks hurt, KEEP WATCH otherwise.** Photo from a distance, keep back, call and
describe what you can see. Not knowing the species never delays the call.

## After every action card

The same gather step, all of it optional: add a photo (its hidden GPS and time fill the map
pin automatically when the phone allows), or use my GPS, or type the place. Name and phone so
the rescuer can ring back. Then save to this phone and share as plain text to the team and the
group chat.

## For the reviewers

Mark anything wrong, missing or unsafe. Especially wanted: the specialist routing you actually
use (snakes, koalas, bats, raptors, marine), the phrases you use on the phone that calm people
down, and the mistakes callers most often make that a card could prevent.
